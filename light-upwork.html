The search problem at marketplace scale

Finding the right freelancer on Upwork isn't a simple search problem. A client looking for "a React developer for a 3-month engagement" needs to evaluate availability, project history, communication style, rate fit, skill match, and timezone compatibility — simultaneously. The old search experience made them do this manually, linearly, across dozens of profiles.

The result was high abandonment. Clients who couldn't find the right match in their first session often didn't return. The opportunity wasn't just better search — it was reducing the cognitive load of evaluation itself.

"The best marketplace search doesn't feel like search. It feels like having a recruiter who already knows what you need."

Browse Experience redesign

The Browse Experience is the primary entry point for clients who aren't running keyword searches — they're exploring categories, browsing talent, getting oriented. I redesigned this surface from the ground up, introducing:

Relevance-aware listings. ML-ranked results that surfaced the most relevant profiles first, trained on conversion signals rather than keyword matching. The ranking model was already built — my job was designing the presentation layer that made the ranking legible and trustworthy.

Skill match indicators. Visual signals on each profile card that showed clients exactly how a freelancer's skills mapped to their project requirements — reducing time spent clicking into profiles that didn't fit.

Contextual filters. A filter system that adapted its options based on the job category, surfacing the constraints that actually mattered for that type of work rather than a generic list of 40 options.

Browse Experience redesign — ML-ranked results with skill match indicators and contextual filtering

Profile Preview redesign

I also led the redesign of the Profile Preview — the side panel that lets clients evaluate a freelancer without navigating away from search. The old preview was a compressed version of the full profile page: wall-to-wall text, no clear hierarchy, no decision-enabling format.

The redesigned Preview was architected around the client's decision sequence. Lead with the credential that earns trust (top rating, relevant past work), then give the context to evaluate (skills, availability, rate), then provide the action to move forward (invite, message, save). Sequential, not comprehensive.

Designing with ML outputs

Working at Upwork deepened my understanding of how to design interfaces around ML model outputs — a challenge that became central to my later work at 84.51°/Kroger. The model can be excellent and the UX can still be terrible. The design work is translating what the model knows into a presentation that earns user trust — which means surfacing the right signals, being honest about confidence, and never forcing users to take the model's word for something they can't verify.
