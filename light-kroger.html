The problem with AI at enterprise scale

84.51° generates thousands of ML model outputs every week — purchase propensity scores, substitution patterns, category trends, audience segments. The models are world-class. The delivery mechanism wasn't.

CPG brand managers at Procter & Gamble, Unilever, and Nestlé were receiving insight packages as static PDFs and Excel exports. They had to manually cross-reference findings, reformat data for their own systems, and spend days validating what the models were actually saying. The insight was good. The experience of consuming it was not.

"The analyst's job shouldn't be to translate a model into a slide. It should be to act on what the model found."

Designing for a new kind of user

The users I was designing for weren't data scientists — they were commercial teams. Shopper marketing managers, category strategists, brand planners. They understood their business deeply but had no mental model for how machine learning worked or what "confidence intervals" meant in practice.

The design challenge was to surface AI outputs in a vocabulary these users already spoke: business outcomes, dollar impacts, competitive position. Every interaction had to feel like a conversation with a smart analyst, not a dashboard output.

Narrative-first insight delivery — AI findings presented as business stories, not model outputs

The system I built

I designed a modular insight delivery system that could package any 84.51° model output into a consistent, readable format. The system had three core layers:

Insight narrative layer. Every finding opens with a plain-language business headline: "Kroger shoppers are switching from Brand A to Brand B at a 2× rate vs. last quarter." No jargon, no model terminology. The finding is the lead.

Evidence layer. Drill-down panels let analysts explore the data behind any claim — charts, trend lines, supporting segments. Available on demand, never in the way.

Action layer. Each insight connects to recommended next steps: campaigns to activate, products to promote, segments to target. Closing the loop between intelligence and execution.

Working at this scale

Kroger's design environment is complex in ways startup environments aren't. Accessibility standards are enforced across the entire platform. Decisions have downstream effects across dozens of products. The engineering teams work in 6-week cycles with defined scope windows.

I adapted my process to work inside these constraints — running compressed discovery sprints, establishing design tokens that propagated consistently across the platform, and building a component library that any of the 84.51° product teams could extend without breaking shared patterns.
